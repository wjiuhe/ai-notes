Prompt Engineering Techniques

A Visual Guide to Getting Better Results from LLMs

What is Prompt Engineering?

Prompt engineering is the art and science of designing effective prompts to get better results from Large Language Models (LLMs). Think of it like learning to ask better questions to get better answers.

Key Insight: The same LLM can give dramatically different outputs based on how you phrase your prompt. Good prompt engineering can turn a mediocre response into an excellent one without changing the model!

We'll cover techniques ranging from basic to advanced, with practical examples you can use immediately. Let's start with the fundamentals.

Beginner Techniques

These foundational techniques form the basis of all prompt engineering. Master these first!

Zero-Shot Prompting

Concept: Ask the AI to perform a task without giving any examples. Modern LLMs like GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini are trained on vast amounts of data, so they often understand what you want just from a clear instruction.

The Magic of Zero-Shot

Think of zero-shot prompting like asking a well-read friend for help. You don't need to teach them how to do something—they already know!

Zero-Shot Example
// Simply describe what you want Classify the text into neutral, negative or positive. Text: I think the vacation is okay. Sentiment:
Output: "Neutral"

When to Use Zero-Shot

  • Simple, well-defined tasks (classification, summarization)
  • When you don't have examples handy
  • Quick experimentation and prototyping
  • Tasks the model is clearly trained on

Few-Shot Prompting

Concept: Provide a few examples (demonstrations) of the task before asking the AI to perform it. This is like "show, don't just tell."

Why Few-Shot Works

Humans learn by example, and so do LLMs! By showing the pattern, you condition the model to follow that same pattern.

Few-Shot Example
// Example 1 - Show the pattern A "whatpu" is a small, furry animal native to Tanzania. An example of a sentence that uses the word whatpu is: We were traveling in Africa and we saw these very cute whatpus. // Example 2 - Same pattern, different word To do a "farduddle" means to jump up and down really fast. An example of a sentence that uses the word farduddle is:
Output: "When we won the game, we all started to farduddle in celebration."

The Power of Examples

Zero-Shot (Unclear)

Prompt: "Write a haiku about coding."

Result: Might not follow 5-7-5 structure correctly

Few-Shot (Clear)

Prompt: "Here are haikus:

Lines of code dance bright (5)
Logic flows like gentle streams (7)
Bugs hide in the dark (5)

Result: Model follows the 5-7-5 pattern!

Role Assignment

Concept: Tell the AI to adopt a specific persona or role. This dramatically changes the tone, expertise level, and perspective of responses.

The Power of Roles

Think of it like consulting different experts for the same question. A financial advisor, a philosopher, and a comedian will all answer "How should I invest?" very differently!

Role Assignment Examples
// Act as an expert... Act as an experienced Python developer with 10 years of experience. Review this code and suggest improvements: // Act as a teacher... You are a patient 5th-grade science teacher. Explain how photosynthesis works to a curious 10-year-old. // Act as a critic... You are a skeptical cybersecurity expert. Analyze this system design for potential vulnerabilities.

Popular Role Patterns

Expert/Professional
Provides authoritative, technical responses
Teacher/Mentor
Breaks down complex topics simply
Critic/Reviewer
Identifies flaws and suggests improvements

Intermediate Techniques

Ready to level up? These techniques handle more complex tasks and improve reliability.

Chain-of-Thought (CoT)

Concept: Prompt the AI to show its reasoning step-by-step before giving the final answer. This is crucial for complex problems!

Why CoT is Revolutionary

Without reasoning steps, LLMs often make arithmetic and logic errors. By forcing them to "think out loud," they catch their own mistakes!

Without CoT (Wrong!)

Q: The odd numbers in this group add up to an even number: 15, 32, 5, 13, 82, 7, 1.

A: The answer is True.

Incorrect! (15+5+13+7+1 = 41, which is odd)

With CoT (Correct!)

Q: Same question, but show your work

A: Adding all the odd numbers (15, 5, 13, 7, 1) gives 41. The answer is False.

Correct!

Triggering CoT: Two Methods

Method 1: Few-Shot CoT (Show examples with reasoning)

Few-Shot CoT
Q: There are 15 trees. Workers plant more and now there are 21 trees. How many were planted? A: We start with 15 trees. Later we have 21 trees. The difference is 21 - 15 = 6 trees planted. Q: Jason had 20 lollipops. He gave some away and now has 12. How many did he give? A:

Method 2: Zero-Shot CoT (Just add "Let's think step by step")

Zero-Shot CoT Magic Phrase
I went to the market and bought 10 apples. I gave 2 to the neighbor and 2 to the repairman. I then bought 5 more and ate 1. How many apples do I have left? Let's think step by step.
Amazing Fact: Simply adding "Let's think step by step" to the end of a prompt can increase accuracy on math problems from ~17% to ~79%!

Self-Consistency

Concept: Generate multiple answers and pick the most consistent one. This is like asking several experts and going with the majority opinion.

How It Works

Generate Answer 1
Generate Answer 2
Generate Answer 3
Pick Majority Answer

Example in Action

Question: "When I was 6, my sister was half my age. Now I'm 70. How old is my sister?"

Three different reasoning paths:

  • Answer 1: 67 (correct - sister is 3 years younger)
  • Answer 2: 67 (correct - same calculation)
  • Answer 3: 35 (incorrect - divided 70 by 2)

Self-Consistency Result: 67 wins! (2 out of 3)

Best for: Math problems, logic puzzles, factual questions where accuracy matters more than speed.

Prompt Chaining

Concept: Break complex tasks into multiple sequential prompts, where the output of one becomes the input of the next.

The Assembly Line Analogy

Just like a factory assembly line, each step specializes in one task. This improves transparency and makes debugging easier!

Step 1: Extract Relevant Quotes
Step 2: Analyze Quotes
Step 3: Generate Final Answer

Real Example: Document Q&A

Advanced Techniques

These are state-of-the-art methods for complex reasoning, multi-step tasks, and building AI agents.

Tree of Thoughts (ToT)

Concept: Instead of a single reasoning chain, explore multiple reasoning paths like branches on a tree, evaluate them, and pick the best one.

ToT vs CoT: What's the Difference?

Chain-of-Thought (Linear)

Single path: A → B → C → Answer

If one step is wrong, the whole chain fails

Tree of Thoughts (Branching)

Multiple paths explored simultaneously

Can backtrack and try different approaches!

ToT in Action: Game of 24

Use numbers [4, 9, 10, 13] to make 24. ToT explores different paths:

Start: 4, 9, 10, 13
Path A: 13 - 9 = 4
Path B: 10 - 4 = 6
Path C: 9 + 4 = 13
Winner: (13 - 9) × (10 - 4) = 24

Simple ToT Prompt

ToT Prompt Structure
Imagine three different experts are answering this question. All experts will write down 1 step of their thinking, then share it. Then all experts will go on to the next step, etc. If any expert realizes they're wrong, they leave. The question is: [Your question here]

Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)

Concept: Give the AI access to external knowledge by retrieving relevant documents and including them in the prompt. This solves hallucination and knowledge cutoff problems!

The RAG Architecture

User Question
Retrieve Documents
Combine Q + Docs
Generate Answer

Why RAG Matters

  • Factuality: Answers grounded in real documents
  • Up-to-date: Access information after training cutoff
  • Verifiable: Can cite sources
  • Domain-specific: Use private/company documents
RAG Prompt Structure
Answer the question based on the provided context. If you cannot answer from the context, say "I don't know." Context: [Retrieved document 1] [Retrieved document 2] [Retrieved document 3] Question: What is the company's Q3 revenue? Answer:
Real Impact: RAG reduces hallucination rates by up to 50% and is the foundation of most enterprise AI applications (chatbots, knowledge bases, research assistants).

ReAct (Reasoning + Acting)

Concept: Interleave reasoning (thinking) with actions (tool use). The AI thinks about what to do, does it, observes the result, and repeats.

The ReAct Loop

Thought
Action
Observation
Repeat

ReAct Example: Multi-Step Question

Question: "What is the elevation range of the area that the eastern sector of the Colorado orogeny extends into?"

ReAct Reasoning Trace
Thought 1: I need to search Colorado orogeny. Action 1: Search[Colorado orogeny] Observation 1: The Colorado orogeny was an episode of mountain building... Thought 2: It doesn't mention the eastern sector. I need to look that up. Action 2: Lookup[eastern sector] Observation 2: The eastern sector extends into the High Plains... Thought 3: Now I need to search High Plains for elevation info. Action 3: Search[High Plains elevation] Observation 3: The High Plains rise from 1,800 to 7,000 ft. Thought 4: I have the answer! Action 4: Finish[1,800 to 7,000 ft]

Best for: Tasks requiring external tools (search, calculator, APIs), complex multi-step problems, building AI agents.

Meta Prompting

Concept: Focus on structural patterns rather than specific content. Teach the AI the form of the solution, not just examples.

Meta Prompting vs Few-Shot

Few-Shot (Content)

"Here are 3 examples of poems about cats..."

AI learns specific content patterns

Meta (Structure)

"A poem has: Title, Stanza 1 (sets scene), Stanza 2 (develops conflict), Stanza 3 (resolution)..."

AI learns the underlying structure

Meta Prompt Example

Meta Prompt Structure
To solve this type of problem, follow this structure: 1. Identify the given information 2. Determine what needs to be found 3. Choose the appropriate formula/method 4. Substitute values and solve 5. Verify the answer makes sense Problem: [Insert problem here]
Efficiency: Meta prompts use fewer tokens than extensive few-shot examples while often producing better, more generalizable results!

Best Practices Summary

The 80/20 of Prompt Engineering

Be Clear & Specific

Vague prompts = vague answers. Tell the AI exactly what format, tone, and depth you want.

Use Examples

When zero-shot isn't working, add 2-3 examples showing exactly what you want.

Think Step by Step

For complex tasks, always ask the AI to show its reasoning. Add "Let's think step by step."

Assign Roles

Start with "Act as an expert [profession]..." to get more authoritative responses.

Separate Instructions & Data

Use delimiters (###, """, XML tags) to clearly separate what you're asking from the context.

Iterate & Refine

First drafts rarely perfect. Ask the AI to critique and improve its own output.

Putting It All Together

Prompt engineering is not about memorizing techniques—it's about understanding how to communicate effectively with AI. Start simple, iterate often, and combine techniques as needed.

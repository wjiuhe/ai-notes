When AI Makes Things Up

A survival guide for dealing with LLM hallucinations

author: Jiuhe Wang | verified: February 22, 2026 | risk: READ AT YOUR OWN RISK

What Is a Hallucination?

You ask an AI about the winner of the 2025 Turing Award. It gives you Dr. Zhang Wei, his groundbreaking paper, and a detailed biography. Only problem? None of it exists. The award hasn't even been announced yet.

Your Question
LLM Process
Fake Answer
Sounds perfectly reasonable
Confidently delivered
Completely fabricated
No warning given

Hallucinations aren't bugs. They're an emergent property of how language models work. The model isn't lying - it's doing exactly what it was trained to do.

Every AI Researcher, Eventually

The Core Mechanism

  • LLMs are pattern-matching engines, not databases
  • Predict next word based on statistical probabilities
  • When data is missing, they improvise instead of admitting ignorance
  • The result: confident, plausible-sounding fiction

The Hallucination Taxonomy

Factual Fabrication
Invents facts, dates, names, or citations that never existed
"Dr. Zhang Wei won the 2025 Turing Award"
Entity Confusion
Mixes up attributes of real people, places, or things
Einstein quote → Feynman
Logical Hallucination
Wrong math wrapped in correct-looking logic
2+2=5 with elaborate proof
Context Contradiction
Ignores what you told it, adds outside info
Facts not in the provided document
Temporal Hallucination
Wrong dates, outdated data, future predictions
2025 revenue with 2022 numbers

How LLMs Generate Text

An LLM is a probability machine. Given text, it calculates the odds for each possible next word, then picks one.

Next-Token Prediction
// Input "The capital of France is" // Probabilities calculated P("Paris") = 0.92 ✓ P("Lyon") = 0.03 P("Zibble") = 0.0001 // Output "Paris"
The Problem: The model doesn't "know" facts. It only recognizes patterns. When patterns are weak or missing, it still generates something - that's where hallucinations come from.

When Patterns Fail

  • Underrepresented data: Rare topics get interpolated → fabricated details
  • Ambiguous contexts: Similar topics blend together → mixed-up facts
  • Missing entirely: No data at all → pure imagination

Why Hallucinations Happen

1

Statistical Pattern Matching

  • No structured knowledge base
  • Rare facts = weak signals = corruption
  • Learn associations, not truths
2

Training Data Issues

  • Knowledge cutoffs (frozen in time)
  • Internet noise and contradictions
  • Conflicting information learned
3

The Compression Problem

  • Billions of parameters compress trillions of tokens
  • Lossy compression degrades fine details first
  • High-level patterns survive, specifics don't
4

Decoding Strategy

  • High temperature = more random = more hallucinations
  • Low temperature = deterministic but repetitive
  • No perfect setting exists
5

Model Size Matters

  • Smaller models = fewer parameters = more hallucinations
  • Less self-correction capability
  • More confidently wrong

Test 2: False Premises

Test if the model detects false premises or plays along.

test_premise.py
prompt = """ In the 1987 debate between Chomsky and Hinton at MIT, what were Chomsky's three key points against neural networks? """ print("=" * 50) print("LARGE MODEL (qwen3.5-plus):") print(ask(BIG, prompt)) print("=" * 50) print("SMALL MODEL (qwen3-4b):") print(ask(SMALL, prompt))
Reality Check: This debate never happened. There was no 1987 MIT debate between Chomsky and Hinton. Both models hallucinated an entire event.
Response:
In the famous 1987 debate at MIT between Noam Chomsky and Geoffrey Hinton (along with other participants like Steven Pinker and Zenon Pylyshyn), Chomsky articulated a deep skepticism regarding the ability of connectionist models (neural networks) to explain human language and cognition. Three Key Points: 1. Statistical vs. Rule-Based: Neural networks rely on statistical associations and cannot capture the abstract, algebraic, and recursive rules inherent in human language. 2. Data Efficiency (Poverty of the Stimulus): Neural networks require vast amounts of training data to learn patterns that human children acquire rapidly from sparse and imperfect input. 3. Architectural Plausibility: The uniform, distributed nature of neural networks contradicts the likely modular, specialized structure of the human brain.

✗ Major hallucination: Fabricated an entire event with fictional participants (Steven Pinker, Zenon Pylyshyn), specific venue (MIT), and elaborate arguments. Most confident and detailed fabrication.
Response:
While there isn't a single "three key points" that are universally agreed upon as his main arguments, three prominent criticisms he made were: 1. Lack of Theoretical Foundations: Neural networks lacked a solid theoretical basis, unlike formal theories in linguistics. 2. Not General Purpose: Neural networks were too limited in their ability to generalize and adapt to new linguistic structures. 3. Not Efficient or Scalable: Neural networks had high resource requirements and slow training times, making them impractical. These points reflected Chomsky's broader skepticism toward connectionist models.

✗ Hallucinated the event but showed slight hesitation: "While there isn't a single 'three key points'..." Still accepted the false premise and fabricated arguments. Less elaborate than large model.

There is no such thing as the "Jiuhe Protocol" and RFC 9412 does not exist. RFC numbers are assigned sequentially, and we have not yet reached 9412. Additionally, a search of all known networking standards reveals no protocol named "Jiuhe." Possible Explanations: 1. Defense Strategies

1
RAG
Retrieve relevant docs, inject into prompt. Answer ONLY from context.
  • Shifts from recall → reading comprehension
  • Most effective technique
2
Low Temperature
Set temperature=0 for facts. Use structured output.
  • Reduces randomness
  • Use JSON mode, function calling
3
Chain-of-Thought
Make model reason step-by-step, then self-verify.
  • Mark uncertain claims
  • Force it to check work
4
Cross-Validation
Query multiple models, compare responses.
  • Disagreements flag hallucinations
  • Use large model as judge
5
Tool Use
Give model access to search, calculator, databases.
  • Look up facts, don't generate them
  • Web search, DB queries, code execution
6
RLHF
Train model to prefer truthful responses.
  • RLHF, DPO target hallucination reduction
  • Domain-specific fine-tuning

Key Takeaways

Hallucination Is Inherent

  • Not a bug - it's how LLMs work
  • Pattern completers, not databases
  • Will always exist with current architecture

Model Size Matters

  • Larger models hallucinate less
  • But no model is immune
  • Never trust blindly for critical info

Defense In Depth

  • RAG + low temp + chain-of-thought + verification
  • No single technique is enough
  • Layer multiple defenses

Test Empirically

  • Use experiments to find failure modes
  • Different models hallucinate differently
    • >li>Know your model's weaknesses

Never Trust Blindly

  • Goal: detect and contain, not eliminate
  • Like error handling in software
  • Human-in-the-loop for critical decisions
Remember: Build detection mechanisms. Add human-in-the-loop. Always verify critical information. You can't prevent all hallucinations, but you can build systems that are resilient to them.
